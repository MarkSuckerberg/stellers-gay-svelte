Since I'm nowhere near an expert on the subject, as well as I imagine it'll end up sounding a bit whiney, I'll just lead with the actual art and then put my ramblings below it.

[RoawayArt](https://x.com/RoawayArt) is a big inspiration to me, especially as of late (if you're familiar with their old quest series (plural) it should be obvious), and some of the creatures are at least partly based on Pascoe, Pilot, and Ecoa from [Return to Sender](https://questden.org/wiki/Return_to_Sender). Another friend of mine actually shared my doodles with them, and they actually drew Pascoe and Ecoa in response! It was definitely very touching to have inspired it.

Anyways. Onto the more... personal part, I guess. Art and I have never really seen eye-to-eye. It's a slow process and I have such great respect for artists, the time they've spent honing their skill. I used to subscribe to the stupid belief that "natural talent" is a thing that exists that I don't have, but I luckily know that's baloney nowadays. Some people can make doing art look effortless, but that's not because they were just born that way, it's because they've probably spent hours, days, weeks, years, decades practicing, most likely going through the same struggles as every beginner artist goes through. That is to say, if they aren't to this day also agonizing about their art being bad and ugly and unfit for showing in public. I've seen so many people create beautiful pieces of art, only to immediately say how bad they think it is.

But, yes. I think I struggle with art because there's just no way to... quantify your skills. With coding, I know I'm not some prodigy or super-genius, but I can tell that my programs are decently well-written, and importantly, actually function. And if you get something wrong, for the basics, at least, you can pretty easily tell what's going on. For more advanced stuff, you can eventually learn to debug, step through code, all that, to find the problem (or at least find something you can look up to see if anyone else has had that issue for advice on how to fix it yourself). Art is all wishy-washy. You can tell that something is wrong, some people who are well-practiced may be able to even put it into words on-sight, but... it's not something you get immediate feedback for like you would with errors. You have to practice bending what you do into what looks right, which... "looking right" is not an objective thing. You can send your art to someone who is practiced, and they might have advice, but it's bound to be a bit vague as everyone applies things differently. You can't just say "Oh, this circle should be 0.5mm down, this line should curve at a different rate, this hand is 2.3% too large." With coding, you can pretty succinctly put things into words. "You didn't initialize this variable before accessing it. You probably should calculate this value once instead of re-calculating it every single frame. You shouldn't use recursion here, since there's a chance you might end up creating an infinite loop." Even more abstract stuff, like... "You're using inheritance for everything, which does work, but you should consider using [composition](https://gameprogrammingpatterns.com/component.html) instead, so that you can reuse more code later on."

I dunno. I make no claims about my art skill. I feel like I'd just beat myself up about it. However, I do know I am improving, no matter how you slice it. Slowly and subtly, maybe, with plenty of setbacks and plateaus, but I am getting there. And I've been told that's the important thing, so I'll choose to believe that.

Anyways, thanks for reading all this if you did, and thanks for merely looking at the art if that's all you did. This blog is honestly more for me than it is anyone else, as I've found it's rather cathartic. I'll post more art if and when I feel like it.
